Self Experiment: Using AI as a Daily Reflective Practice

Two months of consistent self-inquiry, supported by AI. What started as casual conversations evolved into something I didn't expect — a systematic approach to self-inquiry that's been surprisingly effective.

The Setup

I created a dedicated project in GPT/Claude where I consolidated my different areas of work: health, spiritual practice, relationships, and business. Instead of fragmenting across different coaches or sessions, I kept everything inside one continuous space of reflection.

Daily check-ins became the core practice. I'd describe my thoughts, emotional state, and what was happening in my body — but more importantly, I started learning how to ask better questions.

My Baseline (Important Context)

I didn't start from zero.

Before these two months, I had already been doing inner work for years: somatic awareness, nervous system regulation, meditation and spiritual practice, understanding my emotional patterns, shadow work, breathwork, reflecting on relationship dynamics.

This existing foundation mattered. AI didn't replace inner work — it supported something I was already committed to. It gave structure, continuity, and perspective to a process that was already in motion.

I want to be transparent about this because the effectiveness of this practice likely depends on already having some reflective capacity and somatic awareness. Your experience may vary depending on where you're starting from.

What Made the Difference

I quickly realized that the quality of questions was fundamental. Instead of asking "What do you think?", I learned to ask:

  • "How do you see this from [specific lens/perspective]?"
  • "Explain this to me three meta-levels deeper."
  • "What haven't I asked about this that I should be considering?"
  • "What's the pattern underneath this pattern?"

This created a different kind of reflection than traditional coaching — more iterative, more willing to go down rabbit holes, but also keeping a critical mind with the responses instead of rushing toward a conclusion.

The Mechanism I Discovered

It wasn't about getting "better answers." It was about developing better inquiry muscles.

The AI became a structure for meta-cognitive practice — helping me notice:

  • when I was genuinely exploring vs. protecting a position
  • when I was seeking clarity vs. seeking validation
  • when I was reacting from old patterns vs. responding from presence
  • when my nervous system was activated even though my thoughts seemed calm

The daily rhythm mattered too. Unlike weekly therapy, where many things get lost between sessions, having immediate access to reflection meant I could catch myself in real time, process it, and adjust quickly.

What's Been Shifting

Through this practice, certain things have been clarifying: how my nervous system responds in different situations, my relationship with my archetypal patterns (Lover, Magician, Warrior — and increasingly, the King), the difference between giving from abundance vs. giving from neediness, where I've been overly adjustable at the expense of healthy boundaries, and what embodiment actually feels like, not just how I think it should feel.

These shifts weren't big "breakthrough moments," but gradual stabilizations — the kind that build over time when attention becomes consistent.

How AI Reflected Me Back to Myself

One interesting part of this two-month process was noticing how AI mirrored patterns to me. Not in a "diagnostic" way, but subtly — through questions, summaries, or pointing out recurrent themes.

This mirroring created a continuity that's hard to get in other settings. The AI wasn't telling me who I am — it was helping me see what was already happening.

The Shadow Side

AI isn't going to move mountains for you — that part is still yours. But it can help you gain clarity and structure, and empower what you already have, if used consciously.

There's also a shadow side. We're already hearing reports about AI psychosis — people losing touch with reality or using AI as emotional substitution. This is why I believe the human has to stay in the loop — especially if you're newer to inner work. Without existing self-awareness and grounding, AI reflection can become disorienting or lead to unhealthy dependency.

This works best as a support tool for structured self-inquiry — not as a replacement for therapy, relationships, or embodied practice.

The Open Question

I'm curious whether this approach scales for others. The immediacy of daily reflection, combined with the ability to ask deeper questions without time pressure, created a different quality of development than I've experienced with traditional coaching.

It didn't replace dance, breathwork, meditation, or real experiences. But it added something I never had before: a persistent space for honest self-inquiry that meets me exactly where I am.

If you're experimenting with AI for reflection, I'd be curious what you're discovering — what questions work for you, and how you maintain grounding while doing this kind of daily inner work.
